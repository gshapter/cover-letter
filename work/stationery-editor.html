Introduction

In 2020 I led a redesign of the Papier stationery editor - the step in the buying process where the user gets to add their own text, image, and design flair to the stationery items.

Research

Qualitative research revealed why we were seeing a poor conversion rate on mobile. There was confusion about how to progress, technical issues with touch interactions around text selection, and problems with how browser behaved with on-screen elements. With 75% of traffic on mobile this was something we needed to address.

Approach

I spoke with our engineering team and we decided on a two-pronged approach. They would rewrite the technology behind the editor, while I would work to improve the interactions.

I developed a bunch of ideas, starting on paper (in my Papier notebook of course), and moving quickly to prototyping in Figma and Framer.

This step was particularly important because of the interactivity of the product. It was about how it felt to use, as much as how it looked.

Results

After releasing the updates we saw a 12% increase in completion rates, and a decrease in the number of customer support tickets in relation to issues within the editor, meaning additional revenue and time saved for our customer experience agents!

An unexpected finding

After the initial release, I launched a three-step how-to widget, explaining how to use the editor for first time users. This totally bombed.

This was a big failure!


Our A/B test showed a 15% decrease in editor completions! Customers were confused - the thing looked too much like the real UI. Some people were were trying to interact with it and nothing was happening, so they were giving up. Lesson learned. Instead, we went with a flashing hotspot that gently indicated the editable areas.
